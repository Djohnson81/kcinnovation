A Pivotal Moment

For nearly half a century, Kimberly-Clark had lived with dual businesses and cultures. Loyalties in the company were divided along the two major product lines. On one side was the traditional paper company and on the other side was the up-and-coming consumer products company, offering a promising future.

Darwin Smith

When Darwin Smith became CEO in 1971, he was the first to recognize that consumer products were the key to future growth for K-C and required focus.

 

To enable this focus and strategic shift, he made the monumental decision to exit the coated paper business – a decision that, today, would be as big as K-C deciding to exit the diaper business.

Building on the Foundation

of Consumer Brands

In 1978, K-C launched the Huggies brand into the disposable diaper space.  This was viewed as a highly courageous move, as the company had recently failed in prior attempts under the Kimbies and Kleenex Super Dry brands for diapers.

 

After difficult losses in its earlier attempt in the diaper business, Smith committed the company to this crucial step in becoming a consumer products company. The success of Huggies cemented the company's transformation in the minds of the public and the employees themselves.

 

Huggies’ success set the stage for a series of launches that created new categories, introduced some of the most legendary brands in consumer products, and supported the global expansion of Kimberly-Clark in the pursuit of leading the world in essentials for a better life.

Looking Through

the Times

 

Smith’s total commitment to the diaper business after the disappointment of Kimbies, “was the gutsiest move I ever saw a CEO make,” notes retired director H. Blair White.

 

By 1982, Huggies became the first K-C diaper in eight years to have national distribution, and a year later, Huggies was the leading premium diaper. This continued to open the door for more successful consumer products in the future.
