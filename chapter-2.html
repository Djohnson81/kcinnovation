Leading with

Consumer Insights

 

While Kimberly-Clark Co. was a prosperous manufacturer of a wide variety of paper products, the company, under the leadership of Frank J. Sensenbrenner, saw the need to expand its product line.

Photo: Kleenex window display.

Sensenbrenner saw that the industry was changing and that K-C had to change with it.

In 1914, during World War 1, K-C began making cellucotton to be used as bandages during the war effort.

 

After the war, the company learned that nurses had been using the bandage material for feminine hygiene needs – leading to the launch of the Kotex brand, the first consumer product within K-C, and the launch of the feminine care category.

the Times

Expanding into

Consumer Products

 

Battling social taboos and retail resistance, Kotex had a difficult start to selling or marketing their superior product.

 

In 1923, a new advertising company accepted the challenge. Under Albert Lasker, the ad’s copy became direct enough to spur sales, yet discreet enough to be accepted by leading magazines.

Shortly after, in 1924, K-C launched the Kleenex brand, leveraging the same cellucotton material in tissues for ladies to remove cold cream from their faces as part of their nightly beauty routine.

 

To provide focus on the new and emerging “consumer products,” and to keep it separate from the paper mills, K-C ran the Kotex and Kleenex brands under a subsidiary called International Cellucotton Products (ICP) – and based it out of…  Chicago!

 

 

It wasn’t until 1950 that ICP merged into K-C and relocated to Neenah, Wisconsin.
